Q3 PLATFORM RELIABILITY REVIEW
Prepared by the Reliability Engineering team — internal distribution only

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Overall availability for the quarter was 99.92%, below our 99.95% target. The miss was
driven almost entirely by two incidents in the payments path; outside those windows the
platform held above 99.98%. Mean time to recovery improved 18% quarter over quarter, from
44 minutes in Q2 to 36 minutes in Q3, reflecting better runbooks and faster paging. Error
budget for the quarter was exhausted on the payments service but remained healthy for every
other tier. No data loss occurred in any incident, and no customer-facing data integrity
issues were reported.

SECTION 1 — INCIDENTS

We recorded two SEV-1 incidents and eleven SEV-3 incidents this quarter. The two SEV-1s
account for nearly all of the downtime.

INC-204 (payments timeout, 47 minutes). A deploy to the payments gateway shipped a default
connection-pool size that was too small for peak load. Under traffic the pool saturated,
requests queued, and upstream callers timed out. The incident was detected by synthetic
checks two minutes after the regression reached 50% of hosts. Rollback resolved it. Root
cause: missing backpressure between the gateway and the downstream ledger service, so a slow
dependency turned into a full queue rather than shedding load.

INC-219 (cache stampede, 31 minutes). A cache node was cycled during routine maintenance.
On cold start, every front-end replica missed simultaneously and stampeded the primary
database, driving CPU to saturation and slowing all reads. Recovery required manually
warming the cache and shedding traffic. Root cause: no request coalescing on cache miss and
no jitter on the refill path, so a single eviction event became a thundering herd.

Together these two incidents accounted for 78 of the 83 total minutes of downtime. Both
trace to the same underlying gap: the platform lacks backpressure and load-shedding at its
busiest tiers, so a localized slowdown escalates into a site-wide event.

SECTION 2 — CAPACITY

Peak traffic grew 22% quarter over quarter, ahead of the 15% we forecast. Growth was
concentrated in the evening window and skewed toward read-heavy endpoints. Database CPU
headroom at peak fell from 31% in Q2 to 15% in Q3. At the current trajectory we will cross
the 10% headroom line — our action threshold — midway through Q4, before the holiday
promotion traffic lands.

The read-replica rollout that was scoped in Q2 has not yet shipped. It remains the single
highest-leverage capacity investment: modeling suggests it would restore headroom to roughly
35% at projected Q4 peak and would also have blunted INC-219, since replicas absorb the read
stampede that saturated the primary. We recommend shipping it before Q4 promotions rather
than after.

Storage and network headroom are comfortable and not a concern this quarter.

SECTION 3 — ACTION ITEMS

1. Add backpressure and load-shedding to the payments queue. Owner: Payments. Target: early
   Q4. This is the direct fix for INC-204 and the highest-priority item.
2. Ship read replicas for the primary database. Owner: Data Platform. Target: before Q4
   promotions. Addresses both the capacity trend and the INC-219 stampede class.
3. Add request coalescing and refill jitter on the cache path. Owner: Edge. Target: mid Q4.
4. Raise the payments connection-pool default and add a load test that exercises it at 1.5x
   peak. Owner: Payments. Target: early Q4.
5. Review paging thresholds for the read tier; current alerts fired late during INC-219.
   Owner: Reliability. Target: Q4.

All owners are assigned and dates are tracked in the reliability planning board. The team
will review progress at the mid-quarter checkpoint.
